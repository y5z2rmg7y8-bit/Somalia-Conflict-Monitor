[OVERVIEW]
March 2026 witnessed a significant shift in Lower Shabelle's conflict dynamics as al-Shabaab recaptured key territory following its largest operational deployment since February 2025. The group's concentrated offensive in Qoryooley district, particularly around Mubaarak and Daarasalaam villages, combined tactical success with renewed civilian executions, indicating a deliberate strategy to consolidate territorial control through both military pressure and terror campaigns¹²³. This territorial shift occurred alongside continued Federal-SWSPF fragmentation in Bay region, where competing alignments generated persistent low-level clashes rather than the decisive confrontation anticipated in previous months.

[DATA COVERAGE]
ACLED data covers 442 events across all 18 administrative regions, with notable concentration in Lower Shabelle (97 events) and Bari (44 events). IPC data covers the January 2026 assessment period, showing dominant Phase 4 emergency conditions across all regions. CHIRPS rainfall data covers the dekad ending 1 April 2026 (forecast figures), indicating severe drought in Lower Shabelle (52% of normal) and Hiraan (57% of normal). Population estimates derive from UNFPA 2021 projections. Displacement data references September 2025 UNHCR/OCHA Harmonised figures covering all 17 regions.

[THEMATIC ANALYSIS]

**Al-Shabaab territorial consolidation.** Al-Shabaab demonstrated its largest coordinated force deployment since February 2025, concentrating over 200 fighters in Wadaariq village⁴ and subsequently executing a multi-phase territorial seizure operation in Lower Shabelle. The group initially recaptured Daarasalaam and Mubaarak villages on 2 March⁵⁶, then defended these positions against sustained government counter-attacks throughout the month. This pattern represents a departure from the group's recent hit-and-run tactics towards sustained territorial control operations. [Comment: The concentration of forces in specific villages suggests pre-positioned logistics rather than opportunistic expansion, though the data does not reveal the duration of preparation.]

**Civilian execution campaigns.** Al-Shabaab conducted systematic public executions across multiple regions, killing 41 civilians in 15 separate incidents during March 2026. The group executed five individuals each in Saakow⁷ and Tayeeglow⁸, six in Kunyo Barrow⁹, and five in Jilib¹⁰, all accused of intelligence collaboration. These executions occurred in public squares following the group's standard accusation of spying for government forces. [Comment: The geographic distribution and timing suggest coordinated terror campaigns rather than reactive security measures, designed to deter civilian cooperation with government forces in newly contested areas.]

**ISSP operational expansion.** Islamic State Somalia Province (ISSP) significantly expanded its operational footprint in Bari region, establishing multiple bases near Dhaadaar village¹¹ and conducting its largest single attack against Puntland forces since October 2025. The group killed 14 Puntland Dervish Force soldiers across two IED attacks in Dhaadaar and Dasaan areas¹²¹³, marking a tactical escalation from previous harassment operations to lethal strikes capable of inflicting significant casualties.

**Federal-SWSPF fragmentation.** Southwest State Special Police Forces (SWSPF) continued fragmenting along Federal-administration lines throughout March 2026, with competing factions clashing in Qansax Dheere, Baidoa, and Waajid. Federal forces established positions in previously SWSPF-controlled areas, including Baraawe where SWSPF units withdrew entirely via boat to Kismayo¹⁴. However, this transition generated persistent low-level confrontations rather than decisive territorial seizure, suggesting ongoing institutional paralysis rather than clear Federal dominance.

**Inter-clan violence in Sanaag.** Darod-Warsangeli and Isaaq-Habar Jeclo militias engaged in the month's deadliest single inter-clan incident, killing eight people in Waqdariya village on 23 March¹⁵. This followed sustained clashes throughout March over land disputes and clan revenge, indicating complete breakdown of traditional mediation mechanisms. The violence occurred despite Somaliland force deployments, suggesting official intervention capacity remains limited in contested border areas.

[GEOGRAPHIC FOCUS]

**Lower Shabelle.** The region recorded 97 events with 192 fatalities, representing the highest per-capita conflict rate at 14.2 deaths per 100,000 population. Al-Shabaab's territorial recapture of Mubaarak and Daarasalaam villages¹⁶¹⁷ created a defensive corridor that government forces failed to dislodge despite multiple counter-attacks involving SNA, AUSSOM UPDF, and air strikes. According to IPC data, the region faces Phase 4 emergency food security conditions affecting 448,800 people, while CHIRPS data indicates severe drought at 52% of normal rainfall. Combined with 95,994 IDPs already present, these converging pressures suggest acute humanitarian vulnerability in areas where territorial control remains contested.

**Bari.** Despite recording 44 events, the region maintained relatively low fatality rates at 1.3 per 100,000 population, primarily due to ISSP's tactical shift towards IED attacks rather than sustained combat operations. ISSP established multiple bases across Qandala district¹⁸ while conducting coordinated mortar attacks against Puntland positions¹⁹. The group's expansion occurred amid severe Puntland force salary delays, generating multiple protests²⁰ that further undermined security response capacity. With 112,829 IDPs and Phase 4 emergency food conditions affecting 430,730 people, the region faces compounding pressures despite relatively contained conflict intensity.

**Bay.** The region's 41 events generated minimal fatalities but sustained political fragmentation as Federal and SWSPF-aligned factions competed for territorial control. Protests supporting Southwest administration occurred daily between 21-25 March²¹, while Federal forces deployed to key towns including Buur Hakaba²². The confrontation remained largely positional rather than kinetic, suggesting both sides sought to avoid escalation pending political resolution. With 591,660 IDPs - the second-highest burden nationally - and Phase 4 food insecurity affecting 516,904 people, the region's humanitarian situation deteriorated despite relatively limited direct violence.

[TRENDS AND OUTLOOK]

**Conflict intensity stabilisation.** March 2026 recorded 442 events compared to February's 498, continuing the declining trend from January's 630 events. However, fatality rates increased to 530 from February's 677 and January's 870, indicating more lethal but less frequent engagements. Al-Shabaab's 183 events represent a 7% increase from February's 171, suggesting the group is likely to maintain or slightly increase operational tempo in coming months focused on territorial consolidation rather than harassment operations.

**Territorial control shifts.** Al-Shabaab's successful territorial seizure in Lower Shabelle marks the first sustained territorial gain since late 2025, breaking the pattern of temporary occupation followed by withdrawal. The group's ability to hold Mubaarak and Daarasalaam villages against sustained counter-attacks indicates improved defensive capabilities and logistics preparation. This tactical success is likely to encourage similar territorial consolidation attempts in other regions where government presence remains limited.

**ISSP capabilities enhancement.** ISSP's escalation from nuisance attacks to operations capable of inflicting double-digit casualties represents the most significant tactical development in Bari region since August 2025. The group's base establishment activities and coordinated mortar strikes indicate transition from purely mobile operations to semi-permanent territorial presence. This capability enhancement is likely to continue as Puntland forces remain constrained by salary disputes and resource limitations.
