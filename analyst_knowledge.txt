# SOMALIA CONFLICT AND ACCESS CONTEXT FILE
## Standing Reference for ACLED Event Data Interpretation

*Reference date: March 2026. Structural dynamics only. Flag [Verify] where time-sensitive.*

---

## SECTION 1: REGIONAL PROFILES

**Awdal.** Low conflict intensity. Primary dynamic is emerging inter-clan tension between Essa and Samaroon/Gudabuursi clans over political resources in Zayla district. Humanitarian access is broadly open but exposed to occasional clan violence spillover. Event data volumes are low; spikes likely indicate localised clan disputes, not systemic conflict.

**Woqooyi Galbeed.** Somaliland's administrative centre (Hargeisa). Very limited access restrictions. The main analytical risk is conflating Somaliland's relatively stable governance with broader Somalia trends. ACLED events here are predominantly political (protests, arrests) rather than armed conflict. Treat this region as analytically separate from south-central Somalia.

**Togdheer.** Frontline zone between Somaliland forces and SSC-Khatumo-aligned Dhulbahante militias, particularly around Buuhoodle district. Clashes are seasonal (linked to pastoral migration and pasture regeneration) and involve livestock rustling escalating into armed confrontation. Event data spikes in this region often indicate SSC-Somaliland friction rather than AS activity. AS has no significant presence here.

**Sool.** Contested between Somaliland and SSC-Khatumo. The SSC entity was offered formal FMS recognition by the FGS, shifting political dynamics. Armed clashes are predominantly inter-clan (Dhulbahante vs Habar Jeclo). Main access routes between Somaliland and SSC areas have been closed since 2023. Low event data volumes reflect limited humanitarian presence, not stability.

**Sanaag.** Split dynamic: western Sanaag (Ceerigaabo/Erigavo) is contested between Somaliland, NES (North-East State) and Harti clans (Warsangeli and Dhulbahante); eastern Sanaag transitions into Puntland-influenced territory. The Calmadow Mountains host AS presence (classified Very Heavy restriction). Interpret event clusters near Ceerigaabo as political-clan violence; events in the Calmadow range as NSAG activity.

**Bari.** Puntland's northeastern region. Dominant dynamic since late 2024 is the Puntland counter-IS offensive in the Jaceel/Ballade Valley and Almiskat mountains (Qandala district). IS has been pushed into deep mountainous terrain but retains IED capability in previously cleared areas. AS maintains a low profile in the Galgala range but has not conducted verified attacks since 2023. ACLED events coded as IS are concentrated in Qandala district; any IS events coded outside Bari warrant scrutiny. US AFRICOM and UAE drone strikes are frequent in this region.

**Nugaal.** Puntland's interior. Relatively stable with periodic clan tensions. Low event volumes. Events here are typically isolated clan disputes or political tensions related to Puntland-FGS or Puntland-NES competition. Not a significant AS or IS operating area.

**Mudug.** Split between Puntland (north) and Galmudug (south) at Galkayo. Recurrent inter-clan violence between Leelkase (Puntland-aligned) and Habar Gidir/Sacad (Galmudug-aligned) in Galdogob district, with 337+ casualties since June 2024. Southern Mudug districts are exposed to AS influence extending from Galgaduud. The Galkayo north-south split means event data from this region may reflect two entirely different security environments. Any event spike in Galkayo city signals potential escalation risk to wider Puntland-Galmudug conflict.

**Galgaduud.** Contested territory. AS maintains a long-standing control zone, particularly in Ceelbuur district and rural areas. Government forces (including remobilised ASWJ elements) have not retaken AS-held territory despite intermittent offensives from Dhusamareeb. AS posture in Galgaduud is influenced by its fortunes in Hirshabelle; territorial gains there may embolden operations toward Xarardheere and Ceeldheer. ACLED data will show low-frequency but structurally significant events. Under-reporting is severe in AS-held areas.

**Hiraan.** Active frontline. AS shifted to attritional, positional warfare here from mid-2025, capturing settlements along the Bulo Burde-Moqokori-Maxaas corridor. Macawisley (clan militia) resistance has been strained by sustained AS pressure since 2022. Key interpretive point: AS has adopted slower, grinding tactics in Hiraan (sustained multi-day engagements) rather than the rapid advances seen in Middle Shabelle. Massive civilian displacement follows AS advances. AMRF and humanitarian reporting are sparse from AS-held areas. Beledweyne town remains accessible by air but road access is severely constrained.

**Middle Shabelle.** Highest incident concentration in Somalia during 2025. AS territorial gains since February 2025 reversed most 2022-2023 government advances, particularly east of Jowhar. The Balcad-Jowhar corridor is heavily IED-contaminated and subject to AS mobile checkpoints. AS linked its Middle Shabelle control zone westward to Galmudug. Jowhar airfield (three weekly UNHAS flights) is the critical humanitarian lifeline. ACLED data from this region will be dominated by battles, explosions/remote violence (IEDs, drone strikes) and strategic developments. High data volumes here are genuine and reflect active conflict.

**Banadir (Mogadishu).** Capital region. AS conducts periodic high-profile attacks (SVBIEDs, 107mm rockets against AAIA compound, targeted assassinations) but overall attack volumes have declined since Q2 2023. The shift is toward lower-frequency, higher-consequence events. Checkpoint friction, forced evictions and election-related lockdowns also generate events. ACLED data from Banadir is relatively well-reported. Political violence events (protests, riots) increase around election periods. Do not interpret declining AS event counts as reduced AS capability; the group retains urban attack capacity.

**Lower Shabelle.** Persistent IED belts and SVBIED use along Afgooye-Bariire-Awdheegle corridor. AS controls significant rural territory, particularly bridge towns along the Shabelle River. Bilateral UPDF troops deployed from mid-2025 for joint SNA-UPDF counteroffensives. Events coded as battles in this region often involve multiple armed actor types (SNA, AUSSOM/UPDF, AS). AS has destroyed bridges and flooded marshland to prevent government advances. The corridor between Mogadishu and Baidoa transits through this region and is a persistent chokepoint.

**Bay.** Baidoa is the SWS capital and a major humanitarian hub. AS maintains control of most areas beyond 10km from Baidoa town. The commercial blockade on Bay and Bakool towns was partially lifted in mid-2025, allowing local traders to supply goods but not humanitarian personnel or project implementation. IED events around Baidoa increased in late 2025 with AS shifting toward urban harassment. Baidoa is also a site of FGS-SWS political tension. [Verify: FGS-SWS military confrontation in Baidoa was active in late March 2026.]

**Bakool.** Shares Bay's dynamics but is more remote. Towns such as Xudur, Waajid and Elberde are accessible primarily by air. AS controls surrounding rural areas. Limited humanitarian presence means low event data volumes. Commercial traffic has partially resumed following blockade relaxation, but this does not equate to humanitarian access.

**Gedo.** Complex three-way dynamic: FGS-Jubaland political contestation, AS activity, and inter-clan violence (particularly in Luuq district, where Reer Hassan/Gabaawayn vs Ma'alim Wayne clashes displaced 165,000 people since July 2024). FGS-Jubaland friction introduces competing clearance requirements for humanitarian actors. AS cells in Gedo remain more active than counterparts in Lower Juba, with night-time probing attacks on SNA and ENDF bases in Baardheere, Ceel Waaq, Beled Xaawo and Garbaharey. ENDF maintains bilateral presence in Gedo. Event data from this region requires disaggregation between inter-state political violence, clan conflict and AS operations.

**Middle Juba.** Under near-total AS control (Bu'aale, Saakow, Jilib) for over fifteen years. Humanitarian reach is effectively zero. ACLED data from this region is minimal and unreliable. Any events coded here are high-significance signals. This is a reporting black hole; absence of data is not absence of conflict.

**Lower Juba.** Kismayo is accessible. Anti-AS operations intensified in Q4 2025 to the highest level in two years. Roadside IEDs on the Kismayo-Afmadow axis are a consistent AS tool, including civilian casualties. KDF withdrawal from Afmadow (transitioning to Jubaland forces) may reduce airstrip security. AS uses abductions to enforce governance and deter collaboration with government. Events coded in Lower Juba will cluster around the Kismayo-Afmadow MSR and Jubaland force operations.

## SECTION 2: ACTOR PROFILES

**Al-Shabaab (AS).** Operates across south-central Somalia with the widest geographical footprint of any armed actor. Tactical repertoire includes IEDs (roadside, remote-controlled, vehicle-borne, body-borne), indirect fire (mortars, rockets), small arms engagements, complex attacks (combining multiple typologies), targeted assassinations and abductions. In 2025, AS shifted toward larger-scale offensive operations, capturing territory in Hirshabelle through both rapid advances (Middle Shabelle) and attritional warfare (Hiraan). ACLED codes AS events under "Al Shabaab" across multiple event types: battles, explosions/remote violence, violence against civilians. Pitfall: AS-controlled areas generate very few events because humanitarian and media reporting infrastructure is absent. Low event counts in known AS zones (Middle Juba, rural Bay/Bakool, Ceelbuur) indicate reporting gaps, not peace.

**Islamic State Somalia Province (ISSP/IS).** Confined almost exclusively to Bari region (Puntland), specifically the Almiskat mountains and Ballade Valley in Qandala district. Much smaller than AS. Sustained Puntland offensive since late 2024 has degraded territorial control but IS retains IED and ambush capability. ACLED codes IS events under "Islamic State (Somalia)". Pitfall: IS events outside Bari region are extremely rare and should be verified. Any IS event coded in south-central Somalia warrants scrutiny as possible miscoding.

**Somali National Army (SNA).** Federal military force. Operates across south-central Somalia, often in coordination with AUSSOM, ENDF or clan militias (Macawisley). Danab (SNA special forces, US-trained) conducts targeted raids, particularly in Jubaland. SNA capacity varies significantly by region and is heavily dependent on AUSSOM logistics and air support. ACLED codes SNA events under "Military Forces of Somalia". Pitfall: SNA operations are frequently supported by clan militia or ENDF bilateral forces not separately coded, making force attribution unreliable.

**AUSSOM (formerly ATMIS).** AU mission transitioning from ATMIS since January 2025. Troop-contributing countries: Burundi (BNDF, Middle Shabelle), Uganda (UPDF, Lower Shabelle), Kenya (KDF, Lower Juba), Ethiopia (ENDF, Gedo/Bay/Bakool/Hiraan), Djibouti, Egypt (incoming, delayed). AUSSOM posture is largely defensive and static with limited patrols. Operational footprint is concentrated around Forward Operating Bases (FOBs) and airfields. ACLED codes AUSSOM under "AMISOM" or "African Union". Pitfall: AUSSOM force composition changes (TCC rotations, bilateral deployments) affect which regions experience security shifts. UPDF bilateral troops deployed separately from AUSSOM are analytically distinct but may be coded identically.

**Puntland forces (including Dervish Force).** Puntland Security Force (PSF) and Dervish (Darwish) paramilitary operate in Puntland territory, focused on counter-IS operations in Bari and inter-clan security in Mudug. Receive US AFRICOM and UAE support. ACLED codes under "Puntland Forces" or similar. Pitfall: Puntland forces occasionally operate in disputed territories (Mudug, Sool) where their presence is politically contested. Events involving Puntland forces in Galmudug or SSC areas carry political significance beyond the tactical.

**South-West State forces and Jubaland forces.** FMS security forces with variable capacity and loyalty. SWS Special Police Forces operate around Baidoa. Jubaland forces (JLF) operate in Lower Juba and Gedo. Both are intertwined with clan politics. ACLED codes these as state forces. Pitfall: in Gedo, distinguishing between JLF, SNA and ENDF operations is analytically difficult. FGS-Jubaland political friction means some "security operations" may reflect inter-state competition rather than counter-AS activity.

**Clan militias (Macawisley and others).** Community-mobilised militias, most prominently Macawisley (Hawaadle clan) in Hiraan and Abgaal-aligned groups in Middle Shabelle. Played a central role in the 2022-2023 anti-AS offensive but have since been strained by sustained AS pressure. ACLED typically codes these as "communal militia" or by clan name. Pitfall: clan militia activity can appear as both anti-AS conflict and inter-clan violence depending on context. The same militias fighting AS may simultaneously engage in land disputes with neighbouring clans. Disaggregate by location and counterpart.

**International forces.** US AFRICOM conducts drone strikes against AS (south-central) and IS (Bari). Turkey maintains a military base in Mogadishu (Turksom) with special forces focused on protecting Turkish assets. UAE provides material and drone support to Puntland. ACLED codes US strikes under "Military Forces of the United States" as remote violence. Pitfall: US strikes in ACLED data do not distinguish between unilateral US operations and strikes supporting SNA/Puntland ground operations. Strike patterns often precede or accompany ground force movements.

## SECTION 3: SEASONAL BASELINES

**Gu rains (April-June).** Primary rainy season. Riverine flooding in Middle Shabelle and Hiraan (Shabelle River) and parts of Lower Shabelle closes roads, isolates communities and delays humanitarian response by up to 10 days. Movement restrictions increase approximately 30 percent during Gu due to flooding, checkpoint taxation and NSAG road closures. AS typically reduces vehicle-borne operations in flooded riverine areas but maintains foot-mobile operations on higher ground and continues IED emplacement. Gu harvest (July) is the main agricultural output; a failed Gu triggers food security crisis in the subsequent lean season (July-September). Pastoral displacement follows pasture availability; herder movement into contested areas can trigger clan violence over grazing and water (observed in Togdheer, Sool).

**Deyr rains (October-December).** Secondary rainy season. Flooding risk concentrated in the same riverine areas as Gu but typically lower volume. Deyr failure (as occurred in 2025) triggers drought conditions during the subsequent Jilaal dry season (January-March) and compounds food insecurity through the next Gu. Failed Deyr followed by drought drives displacement from rural areas to IDP settlements in urban centres (Baidoa, Mogadishu/Kahda, Beledweyne), increasing pressure on already strained services.

**Jilaal dry season (January-March).** Hot, dry period. Water scarcity peaks. Pastoralists migrate to permanent water points, concentrating populations and increasing clan friction. Conflict tempo by armed groups (AS, government) may increase as roads become passable. This is typically when AS mounts offensive operations (the 2025 Hirshabelle offensive began in February).

**Hagaa dry season (July-September).** Secondary dry period. Agricultural lean season in south-central Somalia. Livestock migration to dry-season grazing areas. Road access generally better than during rains. AS operational tempo varies but the group has historically used this period for consolidation following Gu-season advances.

**Deviation flag.** If ACLED data shows a sharp increase in AS battle events in riverine areas during peak Gu flooding (May-June), this likely indicates a deliberate offensive rather than routine activity. Conversely, a drop in government operations during Jilaal (when conditions favour movement) may signal political or resource constraints rather than tactical choice.

## SECTION 4: HISTORICAL CONTEXT

**FGS-FMS structural tension.** The Federal Government and Federal Member States (Puntland, Jubaland, Galmudug, Hirshabelle, South-West State, North-East State) contest the distribution of power, revenue and security authority. This is not a single dispute but a permanent structural feature. Puntland and Jubaland are aligned in opposition to the current FGS; Galmudug, Hirshabelle and SWS broadly support the FGS but their loyalty is contingent. When ACLED data shows security force events in FMS capitals or along inter-state boundaries (particularly Gedo), assess whether the event reflects counter-AS operations or inter-state political friction. The 2026 federal election cycle intensifies this dynamic.

**Counter-AS offensive trajectory.** The FGS launched a major counter-AS offensive in mid-2022 with SNA, ATMIS/AUSSOM and Macawisley forces. The offensive achieved significant territorial gains in Hiraan, Galgaduud and Middle Shabelle through 2022-2023, then stalled. From early 2025, AS reversed most gains in Hirshabelle. The offensive has not resumed at scale. Government forces now hold district capitals and airfields but lack capacity to control surrounding rural areas. The pattern across south-central Somalia is government control of towns within a 10-15km radius, with AS controlling or contesting everything beyond. ACLED battle data should be interpreted against this backdrop: government-coded events clustered around towns indicate defensive posture, not offensive momentum.

**AUSSOM transition.** AUSSOM replaced ATMIS in January 2025 under UNSCR 2767, shifting from peace enforcement to stabilisation. The transition has been repeatedly delayed. AUSSOM remains 50-60 percent funded. The planned expansion from 23 to 41 bases was not implemented. The BNDF-to-EAF transition in Middle Shabelle has stalled. AUSSOM's mandate runs to end-2027, but financial sustainability is uncertain. The operational consequence for ACLED interpretation: AUSSOM events will decrease over time as the mission contracts. Any increase in AS events in areas previously covered by AUSSOM FOBs signals exploitation of security vacuums. UNHAS relies on AUSSOM for airfield security at seven key locations.

**Somaliland-Somalia and SSC-Khatumo dynamics.** Somaliland claims sovereignty over the former British Somaliland territory (Woqooyi Galbeed, Togdheer, Sanaag, Sool, Awdal). SSC-Khatumo (primarily Dhulbahante clan) rejected Somaliland authority and received FGS recognition as a basis for FMS status. NES was subsequently established. Israel recognised Somaliland in late December 2025, potentially reactivating territorial disputes. [Verify: further international recognitions may have occurred since December 2025.] ACLED data from northern regions (Sool, Togdheer, Sanaag) reflects this political-clan dynamic, not AS-type insurgency. Code these events separately in any south-central Somalia analysis. Any ACLED event in Buuhoodle, Laas Caanood or Ceerigaabo is almost certainly SSC-Somaliland related.

**Clan dynamics in ACLED data.** Clan is the primary social organising principle and shapes all armed actor behaviour. Clan militias appear in ACLED as communal militias. Key patterns: land and water disputes generate recurring localised violence (Mudug, Gedo, Middle Shabelle); revenge cycles escalate rapidly and can persist for years (Galdogob conflict since June 2024); clan affiliations within state security forces affect where those forces will and will not operate; AS exploits inter-clan grievances to recruit and to prevent clan unity against them. When interpreting communal militia events, check whether they co-occur with AS advances (suggesting AS manipulation) or are independent (suggesting resource/political disputes). Seasonal pastoral migration is a consistent trigger for clan violence.
